CS Capstone Project: CNN Redesign

CNN Redesign

Problem Statement

This semester for my ITIS 5390 course we were tasked with conducting a critical examination of a webpage. Working with a team of 4 other students we collaborated to redesign the CNN News Webpage. We found that the original CNN homepage showed significant usability challenges that directly affected ability to quick scanning, clear hierarchy, and predictable navigation. The layout was dense and visually overwhelming, with inconsistent font sizes, redundant headlines, and minimal whitespace that made it difficult for users to distinguish primary stories from secondary content. Navigation pathways were unclear, breadcrumbs were missing, and users often had to rely on the browser’s back button to retrace their steps. These issues created a high cognitive load, especially for users browsing across multiple categories or accessing archived content. During my investigation i found specific issues concerning the cluttered layout, lack of visual hierarchy especially concerning the home page. This formed the foundation for my portion of the redesign where I prioritized clarity, accessibility, and a more intuitive reading experience.

Our Porcess

My role in the project began with a deep heuristic evaluation of CNN’s existing homepage, where I focused specifically on layout density, visual hierarchy, and navigation clarity. The original interface overwhelmed users with inconsistent font sizing, redundant headlines, and minimal whitespace, creating a high cognitive load that made scanning difficult. Navigation pathways were unclear, breadcrumbs were missing, and users often relied on the browser’s back button to retrace steps. These issues really affected readers who browse quickly, rely on accessibility features, or want to browse news across multiple categories. By documenting these issues, I was able to establish the foundation for some potential redesign ideas such as a cleaner, more structured homepage that reduces friction and supports intuitive exploration.

This heuristic evaluation led to some low‑fidelity sketches. For this I took on the task of restructuring the search function of the application. My sketch introduced a search system that reduces cognitive load through a progressive disclosure style design method. My low‑fidelity sketch introduces a dynamic category system that appears only when a user’s search query has relevant results. This eliminates the need to scan the site’s full navigation structure which prevents the users from becoming overwhelmed.

I then moved on to some low-fi wireframes on figma. For this I took on the home page where I introduced a card‑based layout, consistent headline sizing, a reorganized navigation bar that reduced clutter, and a more intentional hierarchy. By using card containers and more uniform spacing,and visual grouping I was able to create a more scannable interface. Our team also designed the search overlay, dark mode toggle, and homepage components that became core elements of the final system. Throughout this process, I iterated based on team discussions and user testing to ensure that design decisions directly addressed the pain points seen by users and usability issues uncovered in the initial critique.

As the low‑fidelity sketches became more refined, I translated them into fully interactive high-fidelity prototypes in Figma. This stage focused on replacing placeholder text and imagery with realistic content, tightening the layout, and defining how each section of the homepage would behave in context. Moving into prototyping was especially valuable because it allowed us to test the actual flow of the redesigned homepage rather than just its structure. Users could interact with the search system, navigate through dynamic categories, and experience the updated hierarchy in motion. This made our user testing far more accurate and helped us validate whether the improvements we proposed, like reduced cognitive load and clearer navigation, held up when people were actually using the interface. The prototypes ultimately became a crucial step between our initial heuristic findings and our development of the site.

For the development phase, I was responsible for coding the video pages and the interactive login/sign‑up form, ensuring both aligned with the Figma prototype and met all assignment requirements. The video pages included responsive layouts, breadcrumb navigation, and linked related articles. I also implemented custom playback behavior so the live video feature would auto‑play on opening, while standard video pages required a user click. For the form, I built all necessary pages, including a sign‑up flow for new users and a login page for returning users. I also added hover states throughout the site to give users clear visual cues when interacting with clickable elements. These coded components brought the prototype to life and demonstrated how the redesigned CNN experience functions in a real environment.

Testing and insites

To evaluate the redesign, we conducted peer usability testing on both low‑fidelity sketches and high‑fidelity prototypes. Users consistently reported that the new layout felt cleaner, easier to scan, and more intuitive than the original CNN homepage. However, testing also revealed areas for improvement: the search overlay’s scrollbar felt unnecessary, trending news needed more prominence, and dark mode should be available across all pages. Users also expressed confusion about the live video login requirement and requested clearer navigation pathways for subcategories. Based on this feedback, I refined the homepage layout, increased whitespace, removed the search overlay scrollbar, added a trending news banner, expanded dark mode across all screens, and improved navigation clarity. These iterations strengthened the overall usability and ensured the design aligned with real user expectations.

Final product

The final product is a fully redesigned CNN experience that is cleaner, more accessible, and easier to navigate across devices. The homepage now features a structured card‑based layout, consistent hierarchy, and balanced whitespace that supports fast scanning. The search overlay provides instant suggestions and clear feedback, while dark mode is available site‑wide for improved accessibility. I also implemented the coded video pages and the interactive login/sign‑up form, complete with real‑time validation, inline error messages, and session consistency. These coded components bring the prototype to life and demonstrate how the redesigned system functions in a real environment. The result is a modern, responsive, and user‑centered news experience that addresses the core issues identified at the start of the project.
